# Case 27: National Power Corporation v. Benguet Electric Cooperative (BENECO) (2021) | G.R. No. 218378

# National Power Corporation v. BENECO (2021) | G.R. No. 218378

The jurisprudence established in *National Power Corporation v. Benguet Electric Cooperative, Inc.* (G.R. No. 218378, June 14, 2021) serves as a critical doctrinal anchor in Philippine civil law, particularly concerning the intersection of contract law, the standard of care required of public utilities, and the precise boundaries of the principle of unjust enrichment. This exhaustive report provides a structured synthesis of the case, directly addressing the contractual framework, the nature of the breach, and the specific Civil Code provisions governing the obligations and liabilities of the parties. 

Key points to understand from this case include:
*   **Contract Supremacy:** The Supreme Court definitively ruled that the principle of unjust enrichment cannot be invoked as a catch-all safety net when a valid contract exists between the parties.
*   **Negligence of Public Utilities:** Government-owned and controlled corporations and electricity suppliers are held to a standard of utmost care and diligence; failure to maintain proper billing equipment for years constitutes gross negligence.
*   **Limited Liability for Underbilling:** A stipulated 90-day correction period in a power supply agreement strictly caps the liability of a distributor for unbilled consumption, effectively acting as a waiver of claims for the power generator for any errors outside that window.

The evidence leans heavily toward prioritizing explicit contractual stipulations over equitable remedies, a principle heavily tested in bar examinations and studied in legal academia regarding obligations and contracts. The following sections break down the case according to the requested structural framework.

## 1. The Parties

To fully grasp the dynamics of this dispute, it is essential to understand the institutional roles of the petitioner and the respondent, as well as the legal framework binding them.

*   **Petitioner (NPC):** The National Power Corporation (NPC) is a government-owned and controlled corporation (GOCC) mandated by law to generate and supply electric power. Within the context of this case, NPC's primary obligation was to deliver electric power and energy to various distribution utilities, maintaining the necessary infrastructure, including the metering systems used to compute the distributors' consumption.
*   **Respondent (BENECO):** Benguet Electric Cooperative, Inc. (BENECO) is an electric cooperative responsible for distributing power to its members and end-user consumers throughout Baguio City and the Benguet Province. BENECO claimed that it faithfully paid its monthly bills based on NPC's meter readings and that it should not be penalized for an underbilling error caused entirely by NPC's internal negligence. BENECO asserted its continued right to a 3% Prompt Payment Discount (PPD) and claimed damages for NPC's threats of power disconnection.
*   **Their Relationship:** The legal relationship between the parties was governed by a franchise and power supply arrangement. Specifically, they executed a *Contract of Sale of Electricity* on January 1, 1998, which was subsequently followed by a *Transition Contract for the Supply of Electricity*. These contracts dictated the terms of power delivery at multiple points, including the Irisan Substation, and established the procedural rules for billing, dispute resolution, and prompt payment incentives.

## 2. Background & Facts

The factual matrix of the case spans several years and centers around a catastrophic billing miscalculation initiated by the petitioner.

*   **What NPC agreed to supply:** NPC agreed to supply electric power and energy to BENECO's multiple delivery points, explicitly managing the metering system installed at the Irisan Substation.
*   **The Power Dispute / What Happened:** In 1999, NPC's Metering Services Group installed the metering system at the Irisan Substation and conducted tests to determine the billing multiplier. NPC erroneously set the Current Transformer Ratio (CTR) at 75/5, which fixed the multiplier at 5,196.31. From the time the substation was energized in May 2000 until February 2004, NPC consistently billed BENECO using this incorrect multiplier. 
    *   In February 2004, an engineer from BENECO (Lawrence Umaming) noticed unusually low systems losses and alerted the National Transmission Corporation (TRANSCO), which had taken over NPC's transmission functions. 
    *   TRANSCO's tests revealed that the CTR should have been set at 150/5. Consequently, NPC had been underbilling BENECO by exactly half of the correct amount of electricity delivered for nearly four years.
    *   On May 13, 2004, NPC issued a demand letter to BENECO for the four-year underbilling, amounting to a staggering P157,743,314.43. When BENECO refused to pay the retroactive amount, citing NPC's sole negligence and the contract's 90-day correction limit, NPC unlawfully revoked BENECO's 3% Prompt Payment Discount (PPD) on June 23, 2004, and threatened to disconnect the power supply.
*   **BENECO's Claims for Damages:** In response to the disconnection threat and the revocation of the PPD, BENECO filed a complaint on September 30, 2004, for injunction, damages, and other relief before the Regional Trial Court (RTC) of La Trinidad, Benguet. BENECO claimed that the underbilling was illegal, unjust, and unenforceable, and sought the reinstatement of the PPD. The RTC originally awarded BENECO P1,000,000.00 in attorney's fees, finding NPC grossly negligent.
*   **Timeline of Key Events:**
    *   **January 1, 1998:** Execution of the Contract of Sale of Electricity.
    *   **1999:** NPC installs the Irisan Substation meter with the wrong CTR setting (75/5).
    *   **May 2000 – February 2004:** Period of continuous 50% underbilling by NPC.
    *   **February 2004:** BENECO's engineer discovers anomalies; TRANSCO confirms the wrong multiplier.
    *   **May 13, 2004:** NPC officially demands P157.7 million for the accumulated underbilling.
    *   **June 23, 2004:** NPC unilaterally revokes BENECO's 3% PPD.
    *   **September 30, 2004:** BENECO files a civil complaint for injunction and damages in the RTC.
    *   **September 15, 2010:** RTC rules in favor of BENECO, enjoining disconnection and ordering reinstatement of PPD.
    *   **August 29, 2014:** Court of Appeals (CA) affirms RTC but deletes the P1M attorney's fees award.
    *   **June 14, 2021:** Supreme Court renders its final decision, modifying the liability period based strictly on the contract.

## 3. The Contract or Obligation

The dispute fundamentally required the court to interpret the bounds of the parties' contractual stipulations versus general equitable principles.

*   **The Original Power Supply Agreement:** The governing document at the time of the dispute was the *Transition Contract for the Supply of Electricity*. Crucially, Section 25 of this contract delineated the protocols for correcting erroneous billings. It divided errors into two categories:
    *   *Category 1 (Omissions/Arithmetical Mistakes):* Errors caused by wrong readings, arithmetical mistakes, or omissions. These errors carried a strict statute of limitations: they had to be corrected through a debit or credit memo within ninety (90) days from the date the bill was received. Failure to notify the other party within this 90-day window constituted an absolute waiver of the claim.
    *   *Category 2 (Inaccurate Meters):* Errors resulting from a mechanically inaccurate meter. These could be corrected at any time.
*   **How it was Breached:** The Supreme Court determined that the meter itself was mechanically accurate; the error was human—a wrong multiplier resulting from an incorrect CTR setting inputted by NPC's personnel. Therefore, this was a Category 1 "omission" or "arithmetical mistake." NPC breached the contract by attempting to retroactively enforce a four-year underbilling claim outside the explicitly agreed-upon 90-day correction window, and by subsequently revoking BENECO's PPD as a retaliatory measure for BENECO's justified refusal to pay the time-barred amounts.

## 4. Legal Issues Raised

The Supreme Court framed the controversy around several intersecting legal issues touching upon contracts, equity, and damages:

*   **Was there a breach / Can NPC demand the full amount?** The primary issue was whether BENECO's refusal to pay the four-year underbilling constituted "unjust enrichment" (Article 22 of the Civil Code) or whether NPC's right to collect was strictly barred by the 90-day waiver provision in Section 25 of the Transition Contract. 
*   **What damages were sustained and proven?** BENECO claimed it suffered from the unwarranted revocation of its 3% Prompt Payment Discount. The RTC originally awarded P1,000,000 in attorney's fees to BENECO due to the necessity of litigation caused by NPC's gross negligence.
*   **Were actual, temperate, or other damages proper?** The Court of Appeals reviewed the RTC's award of attorney's fees and deleted it, finding a lack of factual and legal basis to justify the award under Article 2208 of the Civil Code. The Supreme Court reviewed whether BENECO remained entitled to the 3% PPD despite the ongoing dispute.

## 5. Supreme Court Ruling

The Supreme Court delivered a nuanced decision that balanced contractual fidelity with the partial legitimate claims of the power supplier.

*   **On Breach and Liability:** The Supreme Court partially granted NPC's petition but largely affirmed the protective parameters in favor of BENECO. The Court held that NPC was grossly negligent. Because of this negligence, NPC must bear the consequential losses of its own administrative failure. The Court ruled that BENECO is *not* liable for the four years of underbilling. However, applying Section 25 of the Transition Contract, BENECO is strictly liable for the underbilling that occurred within the ninety (90) days immediately preceding the date NPC's demand was received. Since the notice was received on May 17, 2004, BENECO is only liable for the underbilled amount from **February 17, 2004, to May 17, 2004**. 
*   **On Damages and the PPD:** The Supreme Court affirmed the deletion of the P1,000,000 attorney's fees. However, regarding the Prompt Payment Discount, the Court ruled that the underbilling dispute was separate from BENECO's current monthly billings. Because BENECO had continued to pay its regular current monthly bills promptly, NPC's revocation of the PPD was invalid. BENECO was rightfully entitled to retain its 3% PPD.
*   **Disposition:** The CA Decision was modified. The Court declared that BENECO is liable only for the underbilling received from February 17, 2004 to May 17, 2004. The case (Civil Case No. 04-CV-2055) was remanded to the RTC of La Trinidad, Benguet, Branch 63, solely to conduct proceedings with reasonable dispatch to accurately compute this specific 90-day underbilled amount.

## 6. Key Doctrine / Civil Code Hook

The pedagogical value of this case for bar review and legal scholarship lies in its robust application of Civil Code provisions regarding obligations and quasi-contracts.

*   **The Doctrine of Unjust Enrichment (*Accion in rem verso*):** NPC attempted to bypass the contract by invoking Article 22 of the Civil Code: *"Every person who through an act of performance by another, or any other means, acquires or comes into possession of something at the expense of the latter without just or legal ground, shall return the same to him."* The Supreme Court decisively established the doctrinal rule that **unjust enrichment is not a catch-all provision that can be conveniently invoked when a party has suffered a loss, especially when a contract exists between the parties**.
    *   Under Philippine jurisprudence, the remedy of *accion in rem verso* is merely an **auxiliary** or **ancillary action**. It is only available when there is no other remedy based on contract, quasi-contract, crime, or quasi-delict. 
    *   Because the *Transition Contract* expressly provided a mechanism for correcting billing errors (the 90-day rule), the rights of the parties were strictly governed by this contract, rendering the principle of unjust enrichment inapplicable.
*   **Obligations Arising from Contracts (Article 1159):** *"Obligations arising from contracts have the force of law between the contracting parties and should be complied with in good faith."* Because Section 25 of the Transition Contract acted as an explicit waiver of claims if not corrected within 90 days, this stipulation had the force of law. Equity cannot supersede explicit contractual terms negotiated by the parties.
*   **Standard of Care for Public Utilities (Article 1173):** The Court reinforced that an electricity supplier, whether a public utility or a government-owned and controlled corporation (like NPC), is affected with public interest and is expected to discharge its functions with utmost care and diligence. Failing to discover a metering multiplier error for nearly four years, despite having technologically advanced resources, constitutes gross negligence.
