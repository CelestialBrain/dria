# Case 1: Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office v. Mendoza (2023) | G.R. No. 257849

# Analytical Case Report: Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office v. Mendoza and the Law on Obligations and Contracts

The intersection of aleatory contracts, evidentiary rules, and the interpretation of contracts of adhesion is a complex area of Philippine civil law. The Supreme Court's jurisprudence often bridges the gap between rigid institutional policies and the equitable enforcement of contractual obligations. In the landmark case of *Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office v. Antonio F. Mendoza* (G.R. No. 257849, March 13, 2023), the Court was tasked with resolving a high-stakes dispute over a multi-million peso lottery jackpot wherein the physical evidence of the contract—the winning ticket—was severely damaged. This case presents a profound exploration of the nature of obligations arising from games of chance, the burden of proof in specific performance suits, and the application of the Civil Code provisions regarding the interpretation of obscure contractual stipulations drafted by a dominant party. 

* **Key Point 1:** The Philippine Supreme Court affirmed that the presentation of a perfectly intact lottery ticket is not the sole, absolute condition precedent for claiming a jackpot prize; the essential condition is the actual selection of the winning number combination.
* **Key Point 2:** Lottery tickets are considered contracts of adhesion. Consequently, any ambiguity in the rules promulgated by the Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office (PCSO) regarding prize claims is construed against the PCSO and in favor of the bettor, pursuant to Article 1377 of the Civil Code.
* **Key Point 3:** The Best Evidence Rule does not strictly apply when the issue is the *existence* of a document rather than its *contents*. Secondary evidence may be admitted to prove the formation of the contract and the selection of the winning numbers.

### Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office v. Mendoza (2023) | G.R. No. 257849

#### 1. The Parties

- **Petitioner:** **Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office (PCSO)**. The petitioner is a government-owned and controlled corporation mandated by Republic Act No. 1169 to raise and provide funds for health programs, medical assistance and services, and charities of national character through the conduct of sweepstakes, lotteries, and other similar activities. In the context of civil obligations, the PCSO acts as the offeror and the dominant contracting party that unilaterally drafts the rules, regulations, and conditions printed on every lottery ticket it issues to the betting public.
- **Respondent:** **Antonio F. Mendoza**. The respondent is a private individual and a patron of the PCSO's lottery games. In the legal framework of the dispute, Mendoza is the offeree or the adhering party who accepted the terms of the PCSO's aleatory contract by purchasing a ticket.
- **Their relationship:** The parties entered into a contractual relationship the moment Mendoza purchased lotto tickets from an authorized PCSO outlet. This transaction generated a specialized form of contract known as an **aleatory contract**, which is governed by the Civil Code of the Philippines. Under this legal relationship, the PCSO was bound by a conditional obligation: it owed Mendoza the jackpot prize subject to the suspensive condition that the number combination selected by Mendoza matched the numbers officially drawn by the PCSO.

#### 2. Background & Facts

- **What was the situation before the dispute?**
On October 2, 2014, Antonio F. Mendoza patronized a lotto outlet in Brgy. Dacanlao, Calaca, Batangas, where he placed three bets for the PCSO's Lotto 6/42 draw. He utilized the "lucky pick" method, meaning the terminal randomly generated the six-number combinations for him. 

- **What did each party agree to or owe the other?**
By paying for the bets, an obligation arose. Mendoza agreed to the terms and conditions set by the PCSO, which included the rules for claiming prizes. The PCSO, in turn, assumed a conditional obligation to deliver the jackpot prize of PHP 12,391,600.00 to Mendoza if one of his combinations was drawn. 

- **What actually happened that led to the case?**
The following morning, October 3, 2014, Mendoza discovered that one of his "lucky pick" combinations had won the Lotto 6/42 jackpot. However, a tragicomic series of domestic accidents occurred. Mendoza's young granddaughter grabbed and crumpled the winning ticket. In a well-intentioned but disastrous attempt to restore the physical integrity of the ticket, Mendoza's daughter placed a piece of cloth over it and ironed it. Because lottery tickets are printed on thermal paper, the heat from the iron caused the ticket to turn predominantly black. The only details that remained partially visible were the first two digits of the three bet combinations, the outlet details, the purchase date, the draw date, and a portion of the time of purchase. 

On October 5, 2014, Mendoza presented the blackened ticket to the PCSO headquarters in Mandaluyong City. The PCSO instructed him to submit a handwritten account and subsequently an affidavit detailing the incident. On October 20, 2014, the PCSO formally denied Mendoza's claim, asserting that the ticket could not be validated and invoking its strict "no ticket, no payment" policy based on the warnings printed at the back of the ticket. Desperate, Mendoza brought his case to the House of Representatives Committee on Games and Amusements, which held hearings and eventually issued Committee Report No. 717, recommending that the PCSO award the prize to Mendoza because the evidence established his ownership of the winning ticket. The PCSO obstinately refused to comply with the congressional recommendation.

- **Timeline of key events:**
    *   **October 2, 2014:** Mendoza buys the Lotto 6/42 tickets in Batangas.
    *   **October 3, 2014:** Mendoza realizes he won; ticket is crumpled by his granddaughter and subsequently blackened by a hot iron.
    *   **October 5–7, 2014:** Mendoza reports the incident to the PCSO and submits required affidavits.
    *   **October 20, 2014:** PCSO officially refuses to release the PHP 12,391,600.00 prize.
    *   **September 30, 2015:** Exhausting administrative appeals and legislative interventions, Mendoza files a Complaint for Specific Performance against the PCSO in the Regional Trial Court (RTC) of Balayan, Batangas.
    *   **March 29, 2019:** The RTC rules in favor of Mendoza, allowing the use of secondary evidence and ordering the PCSO to pay the jackpot, plus moral damages and attorney's fees.
    *   **October 28, 2020:** The Court of Appeals (CA) affirms the RTC's decision regarding the jackpot prize but deletes the award for moral damages and attorney's fees.
    *   **March 13, 2023:** The Supreme Court (Second Division) denies the PCSO's Petition for Review on Certiorari, affirming the CA's decision.

#### 3. The Contract or Obligation

- **What was the original obligation or agreement?**
The original obligation falls under **Article 1157 of the Civil Code**, which enumerates the sources of obligations: law, contracts, quasi-contracts, acts or omissions punished by law, and quasi-delicts. The source of the obligation here is a **contract**. Specifically, it is a contract of adhesion. 

By participating in the lottery, Mendoza and the PCSO entered into a mutual agreement subject to the PCSO Amended Games Rules and Regulations for the Lotto 6/42. The terms of this agreement were literally printed on the back of the physical ticket, explicitly warning the bettor: 
1. "Keep ticket away from heat, hot objects, oil, water and solvents."
2. "Prizes will not be paid if the ticket is altered, defaced, torn, damaged or has failed any of the validation tests by PCSO."

Under these stipulations, the PCSO owed the jackpot prize to the bearer of the winning ticket, subject to conditions precedent.

- **Was it modified, substituted, or extinguished at any point?**
The PCSO argued that the obligation to pay never legally matured (or was effectively extinguished) because the suspensive condition—the presentation of an unblemished, fully machine-readable ticket—was not fulfilled. The PCSO relied heavily on the "no ticket, no payment" rule, treating the physical ticket not just as evidence of the contract, but as an indispensable element of the obligation itself. 

However, the courts found that the obligation was *not* extinguished by the accidental destruction of the ticket's legibility. The contract was not the piece of thermal paper; the contract was the agreement formed when Mendoza paid his wager and the PCSO terminal recorded his numbers. Because the PCSO's own digital systems had successfully recorded the transaction (the time, location, and the exact winning "lucky pick" combination), the underlying obligation to pay the prize remained entirely valid.

- **Were there any side agreements or modifications?**
There were no side agreements. The dispute strictly centered on the interpretation of the unilateral rules established by the PCSO and whether those rules could absolutely bar recovery when a legitimate winner suffers an accidental destruction of the ticket.

#### 4. Legal Issues Raised

The Supreme Court was tasked with resolving several core legal and evidentiary questions:
1. **Substantive/Contractual Issue:** Are the provisions in the PCSO Rules and Regulations for Lotto 6/42 strictly literal, or are they susceptible to judicial interpretation due to ambiguity? Specifically, is the presentation of an intact physical ticket an absolute condition precedent to claiming the prize, or is the essential condition merely the *selection* of the winning number combination?
2. **Evidentiary Issue:** Did Mendoza sufficiently prove his entitlement to the PHP 12,391,600.00 jackpot prize despite his winning ticket being partially burned? 
3. **Procedural Issue:** Does the Best Evidence Rule bar the admission of secondary and testimonial evidence to prove the bettor's selection of the winning numbers when the original ticket is damaged?

#### 5. Supreme Court Ruling

- **What did the SC decide?**
The Supreme Court, through Justice Jhosep Y. Lopez, denied the PCSO's petition and ruled decisively in favor of Mendoza. The Court ordered the PCSO to pay Mendoza the PHP 12,391,600.00 jackpot prize for the October 2, 2014 Lotto 6/42 draw.

- **How did it reason through the applicable Civil Code provisions?**
    *   **Interpretation of Contracts (Art. 1370 & 1377):** The Supreme Court treated the PCSO Rules and the lottery ticket as a **contract of adhesion**. A contract of adhesion is one where one party (the PCSO) imposes a ready-made form of contract, which the other party (Mendoza) may only accept or reject, leaving no room for negotiation. 
    *   The Court applied **Article 1370 of the Civil Code**, which states that if the terms of a contract are clear, the literal meaning controls; however, if the contract is ambiguous, the intention of the parties prevails. The SC found that the PCSO Rules were deeply ambiguous. On one hand, the rules required the presentation of a physical ticket ("no ticket, no payment"); on the other hand, the rules defined the game as a "number match game," strongly implying that the *act of selecting the winning numbers* was the true essence of the game.
    *   To resolve this ambiguity, the Court invoked **Article 1377 of the Civil Code**, which mandates that the interpretation of obscure words or stipulations in a contract shall not favor the party who caused the obscurity. Since the PCSO drafted the rules, the ambiguity was construed strictly against them and liberally in favor of the adhering party, Mendoza. 
    *   **Nature of the Condition Precedent:** The Court held that the essential condition precedent to winning the prize is the *selection of the winning number combination*, not the submission of an unblemished piece of thermal paper. The ticket is merely the physical proof of the fact that the bettor selected the winning combination; it is not the winning itself. 
    *   **Evidentiary Reasoning (Best Evidence Rule):** The PCSO argued that Mendoza's claim should fail because he could not produce the original, intact document (the ticket). The Supreme Court clarified that the **Best Evidence Rule** (Rule 130 of the Rules of Court) only applies when the *contents* of a document are the subject of the inquiry. In this case, the *contents* of the transaction (the specific numbers generated) were already undisputed and recorded in the PCSO's own central server. The issue was whether the ticket bearing those numbers *existed* in the hands of Mendoza. For proving the existence of a document and the circumstances surrounding its destruction, secondary evidence (testimonial evidence, the partially burned ticket, affidavits, and the PCSO's own terminal logs) is perfectly admissible. Mendoza successfully established his claim by a preponderance of evidence.

- **What was the disposition?**
The Supreme Court **AFFIRMED** the decision of the Court of Appeals with **MODIFICATION**. The modification was simply upholding the CA's deletion of the RTC's award for moral and exemplary damages and attorney's fees, finding no bad faith on the part of the PCSO, as they were merely strictly (albeit incorrectly) implementing their internal regulations. The core disposition ordered the PCSO to pay the PHP 12,391,600.00 jackpot.

#### 6. Key Doctrine / Civil Code Hook

- **State the doctrinal rule this case stands for:**
In a contract of adhesion such as a lottery game, the essential condition precedent for the fulfillment of the obligation to pay the jackpot prize is the bettor's act of selecting the winning number combination, not the mere physical presentation of an unblemished ticket. When the rules governing the contract are ambiguous regarding the conditions for payment, such obscurity must be construed against the party who drafted the contract (the sweepstakes office) and in favor of the adhering party. Furthermore, the Best Evidence Rule does not bar the use of secondary evidence to prove a winner's claim when the existence of the ticket, rather than its contents, is the primary issue in dispute.

- **Which Civil Code articles does it interpret or apply?**
    *   **Article 1157:** Identifies contracts as a primary source of obligations, affirming the legally binding nature of the lottery wager.
    *   **Article 1159:** Obligations arising from contracts have the force of law between the contracting parties and should be complied with in good faith.
    *   **Article 1370:** Requires courts to determine if a contract provision is ambiguous; if it is susceptible to two reasonable alternative interpretations, the court must look beyond the literal words to ascertain true intent.
    *   **Article 1377:** "The interpretation of obscure words or stipulations in a contract shall not favor the party who caused the obscurity." This is the cornerstone of the Court's ruling against the PCSO's unilateral strictures.

- **How does this case fit into or modify existing doctrine?**
Prior to this case, the PCSO frequently relied on strict, literal interpretations of their "no ticket, no payment" rule to deny claims, previously finding support in earlier Supreme Court rulings regarding "Number Fever" promotional cases where strict compliance with conditions precedent was mandated. *PCSO v. Mendoza* significantly softens this rigid stance in the context of state-sponsored lotteries. It modifies the landscape by legally separating the *substantive right* (the act of betting on the winning numbers) from the *procedural proof* (the physical ticket), establishing that a destroyed ticket does not automatically extinguish the contractual obligation of the State to pay the winner if secondary evidence can conclusively prove the substantive right.

#### 7. Memory Anchor

**The "Ironed Lotto Ticket" Case.** 
When a multi-million peso lottery ticket is accidentally burned by a flat iron, the bettor can still claim the prize using secondary evidence because under **Article 1377**, the ambiguous rules of a contract of adhesion are construed against the PCSO; the true contract is the *selection of the numbers*, not the thermal paper itself.
