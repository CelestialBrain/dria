# Case 11: Del Carmen v. Sabordo, et al. (2014) | G.R. No. 181723

### Del Carmen v. Sabordo, et al. (2014) | G.R. No. 181723

#### 1. The Parties
*   **Petitioner (Del Carmen):** Elizabeth Del Carmen is one of the legal heirs of Toribio Suico. Her obligation, along with the other heirs, was to repurchase Lots 506 and 514 from the Sabordo spouses by paying ₱127,500.00, as directed by a final court decision.
*   **Respondents (Sabordo, et al.):** Spouses Restituto Sabordo and Mima Mahilum-Sabordo. They were the transferees of rights over the properties and the parties to whom the repurchase price was due.
*   **Their relationship:** The predecessors of the petitioner, the Suico spouses (along with Flores spouses), had originally sold their rights over certain properties to the respondent Sabordo spouses. A subsequent court ruling granted the Suico spouses (and thus their heirs, including Del Carmen) the option to repurchase two of these lots (Lots 506 and 514) from the Sabordos.

#### 2. Background & Facts
*   **Original debt:** In 1961, the spouses Toribio and Eufrocina Suico, with business partners, obtained a loan from the Development Bank of the Philippines (DBP), securing it with four parcels of land (Lots 506, 512, 513, and 514) owned by the Suico spouses, among others. They defaulted on the loan, leading to DBP foreclosing on the mortgage. DBP later allowed the Suico and Flores spouses to repurchase the properties through a conditional sale for ₱240,571.00. However, they only paid the downpayment and the first monthly amortization, failing to make subsequent installments.
*   **Transfer of rights:** Facing cancellation of the conditional sale, the Suico and Flores spouses sold their rights over the properties to the respondents, Restituto and Mima Sabordo, with the condition that the Sabordos would pay the remaining balance of the sale price to DBP. A supplemental agreement on September 3, 1974, clarified that Lots 512 and 513 were sold outright to the Sabordos, while Lots 506 and 514 were given to the Suico spouses as usufructuaries.
*   **Subsequent litigation and repurchase option:** Restituto Sabordo initiated a declaratory relief action. The Regional Trial Court (RTC) and later the Court of Appeals (CA) ruled that the Suico spouses had until August 31, 1987, to repurchase or buy back Lots 506 and 514 from the Sabordos by paying ₱127,500.00.
*   **Petitioner's action (consignation attempt):** After Toribio Suico died, his heirs, including petitioner Elizabeth Del Carmen, discovered that the Sabordos had mortgaged Lots 506 and 514 to Republic Planters Bank (RPB). Claiming uncertainty as to whom the payment should be made due to the mortgage, the heirs deposited the ₱127,500.00 with the RTC and filed a complaint for interpleader. They sought to compel the Sabordos and RPB to interplead, demand substitute collateral, and release the mortgage.
*   **Claim after deposit:** The petitioner and co-heirs contended that their judicial deposit constituted a valid payment of the repurchase price. However, the respondents and the courts found the consignation invalid.
*   **Timeline of key events:**
    *   **1961:** Suico spouses obtained DBP loan, mortgaging properties.
    *   **Failure to pay:** DBP foreclosed.
    *   **Repurchase option from DBP:** DBP allowed Suico/Flores spouses to repurchase via conditional sale.
    *   **Sale of rights to Sabordos:** Suico/Flores spouses sold their rights to Sabordo spouses.
    *   **September 3, 1974:** Supplemental agreement between Sabordos and Suico/Flores spouses.
    *   **September 13, 1976:** Restituto Sabordo filed declaratory relief action.
    *   **December 17, 1986:** RTC ruled Suico spouses had until August 31, 1987, to repurchase Lots 506 & 514 for ₱127,500.00.
    *   **Toribio Suico died.**
    *   **Heirs discovered mortgage to RPB.**
    *   **Heirs deposited ₱127,500.00 with RTC and filed interpleader complaint.**
    *   **December 5, 2001:** RTC dismissed the complaint.
    *   **May 25, 2007:** CA affirmed RTC decision.
    *   **January 24, 2008:** CA denied Motion for Reconsideration.
    *   **August 11, 2014:** Supreme Court denied the petition, affirming the CA.

#### 3. The Contract or Obligation
*   **Original obligation:** The obligation of the petitioner (as heir of Toribio Suico) was to pay the Sabordo spouses ₱127,500.00 to repurchase Lots 506 and 514, as mandated by a final and executory court judgment.
*   **The dation (or rather, consignation attempt):** The petitioner and co-heirs deposited the amount of ₱127,500.00 with the Regional Trial Court. This was an attempt at consignation, not dation in payment.
*   **Did the dation fully extinguish the debt?:** The Supreme Court ruled that this judicial deposit did *not* extinguish the debt because it did not constitute a valid consignation.

#### 4. Legal Issues Raised
*   Was the judicial deposit of ₱127,500.00 made by the Suico heirs a valid consignation?
*   Did this judicial deposit, in the absence of a prior tender of payment to the respondents, extinguish the obligation to repurchase the lots?
*   Was interpleader the proper remedy for the petitioner?

#### 5. Supreme Court Ruling
*   **What did the SC decide on the validity and effect of the dation (consignation)?:** The Supreme Court denied the petition, affirming the decisions of the lower courts. The Court ruled that the judicial deposit of ₱127,500.00 made by the petitioner and co-heirs was *not* a valid consignation. Consequently, it did not extinguish their obligation to repurchase the lots.
*   **How did it apply Art. 1245 (and other articles) to the facts?:** The case primarily applied Civil Code Articles 1256 and 1257, which govern consignation, not Article 1245 on dation in payment. The Court emphasized that compliance with the requisites of a valid consignation is mandatory. A crucial requisite is a prior tender of payment to the creditor. The Court found that the petitioner and co-heirs failed to make a prior valid tender of payment to the Sabordos. The Court also noted that the exceptions where prior tender is excused (e.g., creditor is absent/unknown, refuses to give receipt, two or more persons claim the right, title of obligation lost) were not present in this case. Furthermore, the Court held that interpleader was not the proper remedy because Republic Planters Bank (RPB) did not make any claim over the consigned amount.
*   **What was the disposition?:** The petition was denied, and the Decision and Resolution of the Court of Appeals were affirmed.

#### 6. Key Doctrine / Civil Code Hook
*   **State the rule on dation in payment under Art. 1245:** Dation in payment (dación en pago) is a special form of payment where the debtor delivers and transfers the ownership of a thing to the creditor, who accepts it as the equivalent of the payment of a debt in money. Article 1245 of the Civil Code explicitly states that dation in payment shall be governed by the law of sales. For dation in payment to be valid, there must be a pre-existing debt, an agreement between the debtor and creditor to extinguish the debt by delivering a specific thing, the actual transfer of ownership of the thing, and the creditor's acceptance of it as full or partial satisfaction of the debt.
*   **Distinguish dation from novation and from sale:**
    *   **Dation vs. Sale:** While dation in payment is governed by the law of sales, its primary purpose is to extinguish a pre-existing debt, with the object alienated serving as the equivalent of the monetary obligation. In a contract of sale, the primary purpose is the transfer of ownership for a price, and there is no pre-existing debt to be extinguished.
    *   **Dation vs. Novation:** Novation involves the extinguishment of an obligation by the creation of a new one that substitutes it, either by changing the object or principal conditions, substituting the person of the debtor, or subrogating a third person in the rights of the creditor. Dation in payment, on the other hand, extinguishes an obligation by performance through the delivery of a specific thing, rather than by creating a new obligation.
*   **Which Civil Code articles were applied?:** In *Del Carmen v. Sabordo*, the Supreme Court primarily applied Civil Code Articles 1256 and 1257, which govern consignation. The Court emphasized that for a consignation to be valid and extinguish an obligation, there must be a prior tender of payment to the creditor, unless specific exceptions are present.

#### 7. Memory Anchor
*   *Del Carmen v. Sabordo* highlights that simply depositing money in court (consignation) to pay a debt is ineffective if you haven't first offered it directly to the creditor (tender of payment) and they unjustly refused.
