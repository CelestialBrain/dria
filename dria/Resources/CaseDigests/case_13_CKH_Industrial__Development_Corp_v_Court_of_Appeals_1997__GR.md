# Case 13: CKH Industrial & Development Corp. v. Court of Appeals (1997) | G.R. No. 111890

# Comprehensive Academic Report on CKH Industrial & Development Corp. v. Court of Appeals (1997)

The following report provides an exhaustive examination of the Philippine Supreme Court case *CKH Industrial & Development Corp. v. Court of Appeals*, G.R. No. 111890, promulgated on May 7, 1997. The case represents a pivotal jurisprudential text in Philippine civil law, specifically illustrating the strict application of the requisites for **legal compensation** under the Civil Code of the Philippines, and its intersection with the doctrine of separate corporate juridical personality. 

*   **Key Points:**
    *   **Legal Compensation:** The Supreme Court affirmed that legal compensation requires strict concurrence of all requisites under Article 1279 of the Civil Code, primarily the mutuality of parties acting as principal creditors and debtors in their own right.
    *   **Operation by Law vs. Consent:** While legal compensation operates *ipso jure* (by operation of law) under Article 1290 without the need for consent, its automatic nature is entirely dependent on fulfilling the stringent mutuality requirements. 
    *   **Corporate Fiction:** The evidence suggests that an attempt to offset a corporate debt using the personal credits of its stockholders violates the principle of separate juridical personality, defeating the requisite that parties must be mutually bound to each other.
    *   **Rescission:** Failure to establish valid payment through compensation triggers the injured party's right to rescind the reciprocal obligation under Article 1191 of the Civil Code.

The ensuing analysis is structured to address the specific factual background, contractual obligations, legal controversies, and doctrinal anchors essential for a rigorous academic and bar-review understanding of this milestone case.

### CKH Industrial & Development Corp. v. Court of Appeals (1997) | G.R. No. 111890

#### 1. The Parties

- **Petitioner (CKH Industrial):** 
  CKH Industrial and Development Corporation (CKH) was a corporation established under Philippine law by the late Cheng Kim Heng, an immigrant of Chinese descent. Following Cheng's death, control of the corporation passed to his second wife, Rubi Saw, who acted as the authorized representative and president of CKH during the litigation. CKH was the registered owner and vendor of two contiguous parcels of land (4,590 sq. m. and 300 sq. m.) located in Karuhatan, Valenzuela. **Their claim:** CKH, as the vendor, claimed an outstanding debt of ₱800,000.00 representing the unpaid purchase price for the sale of its real properties.

- **Respondents:** 
  The primary private respondent was Century-Well Phil. Corporation (Century-Well), the vendee in the transaction, represented by Lourdes Chong. The other individual respondents included Chong Tak Kei and Chong Tak Choi, who were the sons of Cheng Kim Heng from his first wife in Hong Kong, and who were also stockholders/part-owners of Century-Well. Uy Chi Kim was also named as a respondent, acting as the mediator from the Chinese community who brokered the settlement between the feuding families. **Their claim/debt:** Century-Well owed CKH ₱800,000.00 for the land. However, the respondents claimed this debt was extinguished via a ₱100,000.00 cash payment and a ₱700,000.00 set-off (compensation) against three promissory notes executed by the late Cheng Kim Heng (as president of CKH) in favor of his sons, Chong Tak Choi and Chong Tak Kei.

- **Their relationship:** 
  The relationship between the actual contracting parties (CKH and Century-Well) was strictly that of Vendor and Vendee. However, there were **no reciprocal debts** between them. CKH was a creditor to Century-Well for the purchase price of the land. Conversely, CKH was a debtor to Chong Tak Choi and Chong Tak Kei (individuals) for the promissory notes. Century-Well was *not* a creditor of CKH, and CKH was *not* a debtor of Century-Well. This structural lack of mutuality formed the crux of the legal dispute.

#### 2. Background & Facts

- **What were the two debts — who owed what to whom?**
  1.  **The Land Debt:** Century-Well owed CKH ₱800,000.00 as the stipulated consideration for the Deed of Absolute Sale covering the two parcels of land in Valenzuela.
  2.  **The Promissory Note Debt:** CKH (via its late founder Cheng Kim Heng) owed a total of ₱700,000.00 to his sons. Specifically, there were three promissory notes: ₱400,000.00 and ₱100,000.00 owed to Chong Tak Choi, and ₱200,000.00 owed to Chong Tak Kei.

- **Did both debts exist and were they due, liquidated, and demandable?**
  Both debts factually existed and involved sums of money (liquidated and demandable). The ₱800,000.00 purchase price was due upon the execution of the Deed of Sale. The ₱700,000.00 covered by the promissory notes was also acknowledged as a mature pecuniary obligation. However, while the debts were individually due, liquidated, and demandable, they did not exist between the *same two parties*. 

- **Did one party attempt to invoke compensation? Was the other party's debt disputed?**
  Yes, the respondents (Century-Well, et al.) attempted to invoke legal and/or conventional compensation to prove that they had paid the ₱800,000.00 consideration. They argued that the ₱700,000.00 owed by CKH to the sons should offset the ₱800,000.00 owed by Century-Well to CKH. Rubi Saw (for CKH) violently disputed this. She did not necessarily dispute the existence of the promissory notes, but she vehemently disputed that Century-Well could use those notes as a substitute for actual payment to CKH. She claimed she signed the Deed of Sale based purely on the mediator's (Uy Chi Kim) assurance that she would be paid the ₱800,000.00 in cash, which never materialized. 

- **What actually happened that led to the dispute?**
  Following the death of CKH founder Cheng Kim Heng in 1984, a fierce family feud erupted over the control of the estate and properties between his first family (based in Hong Kong, including sons Choi and Kei) and his second wife (Rubi Saw, in the Philippines). Seeking to avoid protracted litigation, the Chinese community mediated the dispute. A compromise was supposedly reached: Rubi Saw would sell the CKH properties to Century-Well (the sons' corporation) for ₱800,000.00. On May 8, 1988, Rubi Saw and Lourdes Chong signed the Deed of Absolute Sale at the mediator's residence. When Century-Well registered the deed and transferred the titles without handing over the cash consideration, CKH filed a complaint for rescission/annulment of the sale for failure of consideration. Century-Well raised the defense of extinguishment of obligation via compensation.

- **Timeline of key events**
  *   **1984:** Cheng Kim Heng dies; family feud begins.
  *   **May 8, 1988:** Mediation culminates in the execution of the Deed of Absolute Sale between CKH and Century-Well at the mediator's home.
  *   **May 9, 1988:** The Deed of Absolute Sale is notarized.
  *   **August 4, 1988:** Preliminary injunction denied by the trial court.
  *   **February 4, 1991:** The Regional Trial Court (RTC) rules in favor of CKH, finding no valid payment, denying compensation, and ordering rescission of the sale.
  *   **April 21, 1993:** The Court of Appeals (CA) reverses the RTC, ruling that the purchase price was validly paid via ₱100k cash and ₱700k compensation (off-setting).
  *   **May 7, 1997:** The Supreme Court (SC) issues its Decision, reversing the CA, rejecting the compensation argument, and reinstating the RTC's rescission of the sale.

#### 3. The Contract or Obligation

- **What were the original obligations of each party?**
  Under the Deed of Absolute Sale, **CKH** had the obligation to deliver the titles and ownership of the two Valenzuela parcels to Century-Well. **Century-Well**, in return, had the reciprocal obligation to pay the purchase price of ₱800,000.00. Separately, under the historical promissory notes, **CKH** had the obligation to pay Chong Tak Choi ₱500,000.00 and Chong Tak Kei ₱200,000.00.

- **Were the debts of the same kind (money vs. money)?**
  Yes, both obligations consisted of the payment of a sum of money in Philippine Currency, satisfying the requirement under Article 1279(2) that both debts consist in a sum of money or consumable goods of the same kind.

- **Were both debts liquidated and demandable at the same time?**
  Yes, both debts were reduced to specific, liquidated monetary amounts (₱800,000.00 and ₱700,000.00, respectively) and were currently demandable at the time of the transaction. The fatal flaw was not the nature or maturity of the debts, but the identity of the obligors and obligees.

#### 4. Legal Issues Raised

- **Are all the requisites of legal compensation present?**
  The central legal issue was whether the requisites for legal compensation under Article 1279 of the Civil Code were met. Specifically, the focus was on Article 1279(1): "That each one of the obligors be bound principally, and that he be at the same time a principal creditor of the other". The petitioners argued that CKH and Century-Well were not mutually bound as principal creditors and debtors of each other.

- **Does legal compensation operate automatically or only upon mutual agreement?**
  Legal compensation, by doctrinal definition under Article 1290 of the Civil Code, operates automatically by operation of law (*ipso jure*). From the moment all requisites in Article 1279 concur, compensation takes effect automatically and extinguishes both debts to the concurrent amount, even without the consent or knowledge of the parties. Conversely, *conventional* or voluntary compensation requires the mutual agreement of the parties to extinguish their respective credits. Because the strict legal requisites (specifically mutuality) were missing, automatic legal compensation could not occur. Furthermore, the SC found no evidence that CKH (via Rubi Saw) ever actually consented to a *conventional* compensation, as she consistently demanded cash payment.

- **Is a disputed debt subject to legal compensation?**
  Under Article 1279(5) of the Civil Code, a debt cannot be subject to legal compensation if there is any "retention or controversy, commenced by third persons and communicated in due time to the debtor". Furthermore, while an unliquidated or highly disputed debt between the parties themselves generally prevents automatic legal compensation, the Supreme Court's primary focus in this specific case was not on whether the promissory notes were disputed in amount, but rather that they were held by the wrong legal entities (the individual stockholders rather than the contracting corporation).

#### 5. Supreme Court Ruling

- **What did the SC hold on the requisites of legal compensation?**
  The Supreme Court ruled firmly that **there could be no valid legal compensation** because the paramount requisite of mutuality was absent. The Court held that CKH and Century-Well, the principal contracting parties to the sale, were not mutually bound as creditors and debtors in their own names. 

- **How did it treat the disputed or unliquidated nature of one of the debts?**
  The Court bypassed an intensive inquiry into whether the promissory notes were valid or fabricated (a point of factual contention) by resting its decision on an absolute barrier of corporate law: **the doctrine of separate juridical personality**. The promissory notes evidenced a debt of CKH to Chong Tak Choi and Chong Tak Kei in their personal capacities. These two individuals were merely stockholders of Century-Well. The Court held that a corporation has a personality separate and distinct from its stockholders. Therefore, the personal credits of the stockholders (the sons) cannot be used to legally offset the corporate debt of Century-Well to CKH. Piercing the corporate veil was deemed utterly unjustified as Century-Well was a distinct party.

- **What was the disposition?**
  The Supreme Court granted the petition, reversed and set aside the decision of the Court of Appeals, and reinstated the trial court's decision ordering the rescission/annulment of the Deed of Absolute Sale. Because no valid compensation occurred and no cash was paid, there was a total failure of consideration. The Register of Deeds was ordered to cancel the transfer certificates of title issued to Century-Well and restore the titles to CKH. (The award of moral damages by the RTC was modified/deleted based on other evidentiary grounds).

#### 6. Key Doctrine / Civil Code Hook

- **State the requisites of legal compensation under Art. 1279**
  Under **Article 1279 of the Civil Code**, in order that legal compensation may be proper, it is necessary:
  1.  That each one of the obligors be bound principally, and that he be at the same time a principal creditor of the other;
  2.  That both debts consist in a sum of money, or if the things due are consumable, they be of the same kind, and also of the same quality if the latter has been stated;
  3.  That the two debts be due;
  4.  That they be liquidated and demandable;
  5.  That over neither of them there be any retention or controversy, commenced by third persons and communicated in due time to the debtor.

- **State the rule that legal compensation operates by operation of law (Art. 1290) — no consent needed**
  Pursuant to **Article 1290**, when all the requisites mentioned in Article 1279 are present, compensation takes effect by operation of law (*ipso jure*), and extinguishes both debts to the concurrent amount, even though the creditors and debtors are not aware of the compensation. Consent of the parties is absolutely not required. However, as demonstrated in *CKH v. Court of Appeals*, if the strict requirement of mutuality is missing, automatic compensation fails entirely. 

- **State the rule on liquidated vs. unliquidated debts in compensation**
  For legal compensation to operate automatically, the debts must be liquidated—meaning their exact amount is already determined or easily determinable by simple mathematical computation. If a debt is unliquidated (e.g., pending assessment of damages), legal compensation cannot take place. In the absence of the legal requisites (like mutuality or liquidity), parties may still resort to **Conventional/Voluntary Compensation** (Article 1282), but this requires mutual consent and agreement to extinguish their respective credits. In this case, CKH never consented to a conventional set-off.

- **Which Civil Code articles were applied?**
  *   **Article 1231:** Outlining compensation as a mode of extinguishing an obligation.
  *   **Article 1278:** Defining the core concept of compensation (persons mutually creditors and debtors of each other).
  *   **Article 1279:** Enumerating the strict requisites for legal compensation.
  *   **Article 1249:** Mentioned regarding the payment of debts in money and the effect of delivering mercantile documents.
  *   **Article 1191:** The implicit basis for the rescission of reciprocal obligations due to a breach or failure of consideration (non-payment).
