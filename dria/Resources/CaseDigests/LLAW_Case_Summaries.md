## Module 1A. Introductory Cases

### Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office v. Mendoza

Summary: Antonio Mendoza won the lottery, but his granddaughter crumpled his ticket, and his daughter accidentally ironed it black. The PCSO refused to pay his P12 million prize, citing their "no ticket, no payment" rule. The Supreme Court sided with Mendoza, ordering PCSO to pay because his winning numbers were clearly recorded in their system, and the damaged ticket was just physical proof, not the actual win.

LL: Always remember that in contracts of adhesion, like lottery tickets, any ambiguity in the rules is interpreted against the party who drafted them (like PCSO), not the consumer (Art. 1377). The court will look at the true intention and substance of the agreement, not just strict literal interpretations of physical evidence.

### Mother Goose Special School System, Inc. v. Palaganas, et al.

Summary: Rhys Palaganas was repeatedly punched by classmates Noel and Mark at Mother Goose School while their teacher was absent. The school ignored initial reports, failed to promptly inform Rhys's parents, and then conducted a dismissive investigation, downplaying the assault as "teasing" and taking no disciplinary action. The Supreme Court found Mother Goose School liable for failing to protect Rhys.

LL: Schools, make sure you have clear protocols for student violence and investigate thoroughly. Your liability for student safety is contractual (*culpa contractual*), not just a quasi-delict. This means the "diligence of a good father of a family" defense (under Article 2180) won't fully save you if you breach your duty to provide a safe learning environment.

### Tanke v. Development Bank of the Philippines, et al.

Summary: Alejandro Tankeh, a doctor and businessman, signed a solidary loan agreement with DBP for his brother Ruperto's shipping company, SSLI, after Ruperto promised him shares and a board seat. When SSLI defaulted, Alejandro claimed he was defrauded by Ruperto. ## Module 1B. Kinds of Obligations + Art. 1191

### Arco Pulp and Paper Co., Inc. v. Lim

Summary: Arco Pulp bought scrap paper from Dan T. Lim, agreeing to either pay cash or deliver finished products. Arco issued a bouncing check to Lim, then tried to pass its obligation to a third party, Eric Sy, without Lim's consent. The Supreme Court ruled that Arco chose to pay cash when it issued the check, making it a simple monetary obligation. Since Lim never consented to the new arrangement, there was no valid novation, and Arco and its CEO, Candida Santos, were held solidarily liable for acting in bad faith.

LL: Don't issue bouncing checks or try to substitute debtors without the creditor's explicit consent. Once you communicate your choice in an alternative obligation (Art. 1199), that choice is binding, and the obligation becomes simple. Remember, corporate officers can be held personally liable for corporate debts if they act in bad faith, like Candida Santos did here.

### Esico v. Alphaland Corporation

Summary: Jose Esico, a pilot, received specialized training from Alphaland, agreeing to serve for five years or reimburse the pro-rated cost. He resigned after two years, claiming constructive dismissal, and Alphaland demanded the training reimbursement. The Supreme Court ruled that Alphaland's claim for reimbursement was a civil law matter, not a labor dispute, so the labor arbiter had no jurisdiction over it.

LL: Don't file civil law claims, like breaches of training bond contracts, in labor tribunals; they belong in regular courts. Always remember that a training bond with a service requirement creates reciprocal obligations, and its breach falls under Article 1191 of the Civil Code, allowing for specific performance or damages.

## Module 1C. Breach, Delay, Damages, and Remedies (Arts. 1166-1177)

### Limjoco v. Court of Appeals

Summary: Limjoco agreed to sell land to Robert Tan, promising to execute the deed and arrange a mortgage assumption within 60 days, with Tan paying earnest money upfront. Limjoco failed to get co-owner signatures and arrange the mortgage transfer, even though Tan was ready and reminded him. The Supreme Court ruled that Limjoco was solely in delay because he failed to perform his part, making it impossible for Tan to fulfill his.

LL: Always fulfill your end of a reciprocal obligation, especially if there's a deadline. Don't assume the other party is also in delay if you haven't performed your part, especially if they've shown readiness. Under Article 1169, if one party is ready to comply, the other's delay begins, and they can't claim mutual default.

### Arrieta v. NARIC

Summary: Paz Arrieta agreed to supply rice to NARIC, who promised to open an immediate letter of credit. But NARIC couldn't open it because they didn't have enough money for the bank's required deposit. This delay caused Arrieta's foreign supplier to cancel her rice allocation, making her lose her deposit and expected profits. The Supreme Court ruled that NARIC's financial incapacity was not a fortuitous event and ordered them to pay Arrieta for her lost profits.

LL: Don't enter into contracts if you know you can't fulfill your financial obligations, as financial incapacity is not considered force majeure (Art. 1174). Always ensure you have the means to perform your part, especially when time is of the essence. Breaching a contract by contravening its tenor (Art. 1170) can lead to paying for lost profits (lucrum cessans).

### Magat v. Medialdea

Summary: Victorino Magat agreed to import radio transceivers from Japan for Santiago Guerrero's taxi business, with Guerrero promising to open a Letter of Credit for payment. Guerrero then deliberately refused to open the L/C, breaching their contract. Magat sued for damages, including lost profits, but the trial court dismissed the case, saying the damages were speculative. The Supreme Court reversed, ruling that Magat had a valid cause of action for breach and that lost profits are recoverable if proven, especially when bad faith is alleged

## Module 2A. Payment and Consignation

### Domasian, et al. v. Demdam

Summary: Spouses Domasian borrowed P75,000 from Manuel Demdam with an exorbitant 8% monthly interest. Years later, they tried to pay with a manager's check, but Demdam refused it. The Supreme Court ruled that this mere tender of payment wasn't enough to stop the interest from accruing because the Domasian's failed to consign the payment in court. However, the Court did reduce the unconscionable 8% monthly interest to a more reasonable 12% per annum.

LL: Always remember that a mere tender of payment, if refused by the creditor, is not enough to extinguish an obligation or stop interest from accruing. You must follow it up with a valid consignation in court, as per Civil Code Articles 1256-1261. Also, courts have the power to reduce excessively high and unconscionable interest rates.

### Rustia, et al. v. Aguinaldo & Aguinaldo

Summary: Juan and Filomena Rustia owed Aguinaldo & Aguinaldo money for shares. During WWII, the Rustias tried to pay with Japanese war notes but attached conditions, like demanding a buy-out and filing cross-claims. Aguinaldo refused the conditional payment, so the Rustias consigned the money in court. The Supreme Court ruled the tender and consignation invalid because they were conditional, so the Rustias bore the loss when the war notes became worthless.

LL: Don't attach conditions to your payment or consignation. For a tender of payment and subsequent consignation to be valid and extinguish an obligation, it must be absolute and unconditional. Otherwise, the obligation isn't extinguished, and the debtor retains the risk of loss, as per Article 1256 of the Civil Code.

### Gatchalian Realty, Inc. v. Angeles

Summary: Gatchalian Realty tried to cancel Evelyn Angeles's contract for a house and lot after she defaulted, claiming they offset her cash surrender value against "rentals" she supposedly owed. The Supreme Court said Gatchalian couldn't do that because the "rentals" were unliquidated and unilaterally determined. Since Gatchalian never actually refunded the cash surrender value, their cancellation of the contract was invalid.

LL: Make sure to strictly follow the Maceda Law when cancelling an installment contract: you need both a notarized notice and the *actual* refund of the cash surrender value. Don't try to unilaterally offset unliquidated claims like arbitrary "rentals" against a buyer's statutory refund, as legal compensation (Art. 1279) only applies if both debts are liquidated and demandable.

### Province of Bataan v. Escalada

Summary: The Province of Bataan held a tax auction for Sunrise Paper Products' property, which Victor Gawtee had already bought in a separate execution sale. Despite a court order to maintain *status quo*, the Province, in bad faith, colluded with Sunrise to dismantle and sell the machinery as scrap. The Supreme Court sided with Gawtee, upholding the P120M actual damages award and declaring the Province's tax sale void due to procedural errors.

LL: Always ensure strict compliance with legal procedures, especially for tax sales, or they will be void. Never disregard court orders, as acting in bad faith can lead to massive liability. Actual damages under Article 2199 can be proven by strong evidence like appraisals and even the defendant's own admissions.

### Bendecio v. Bautista

Summary: Virginia lent her niece, Julieta, money for a business. When the loan was due, Julieta's business partner, Merlyn, gave Virginia a promissory note to pay the debt. Julieta argued this meant she was no longer liable (novation), but Virginia never explicitly agreed to release her. The Supreme Court ruled there was no novation, so Julieta and Merlyn were both solidarily liable to Virginia.

LL: Don't assume you're off the hook just because someone else offers to pay your debt! For novation to happen and release the original debtor, the creditor MUST clearly and unequivocally agree to that release. Without the creditor's express consent, the original debtor remains liable, and the new person just becomes an additional debtor (Art. 1293).

## Module 3A. Fortuitous Events and Force Majeure

### Agro Conglomerates, Inc. v. Court of Appeals

Summary: Agro Conglomerates and Mario Soriano borrowed money from Regent Bank, but an Addendum said Wonderland Food Industries would pay it. The catch was, this Addendum was signed *before* Agro actually took out the loan. When Wonderland didn't pay and their land deal fell apart, Agro claimed novation to escape liability. The Supreme Court said no, because you can't novate a debt that doesn't even exist yet, so Agro still had to pay the bank.

LL: Always remember that for novation by substitution of debtor to be valid, there must first be a *previous valid obligation* to be novated (Art. 1292). Don't assume an agreement to shift debt before the debt is even incurred will release you. The creditor's clear consent to release the original debtor is also indispensable (Art. 1293).

### PNB v. Mallari

Summary: Hermogenes Mallari got a crop loan from PNB, secured by a surety bond from First National Surety. When Mallari defaulted, PNB sued and attached his palay. Conrado Guanzon, Mallari's employer, then offered to pay PNB in installments if the palay was released, which PNB accepted. When Guanzon later defaulted, PNB tried to go after Mallari and the surety, but the Supreme Court said a novation occurred, extinguishing the original debt and releasing the surety.

LL: Make sure to clearly state your intentions when modifying a loan. If you accept a new debtor, new payment terms, and release the original collateral, you've likely created a new obligation that extinguishes the old one (Art. 1292). Remember, accessory obligations like a surety bond also disappear when the principal debt is novated (Art. 1296).

### Y-I Leisure Philippines, Inc. v. Yu

Summary: James Yu bought golf shares from MADCI, but the club was never built, so he demanded a refund. MADCI tried to escape liability by claiming its president, Sangil, would pay him instead, and then sold all its assets to Y-I Leisure. The Supreme Court ruled that MADCI couldn't change who owed Yu money without his consent, so the attempted novation failed. Y-I Leisure was also held liable because they acquired all of MADCI's assets and continued the business.

LL: Always remember that for a valid novation, especially when substituting a debtor, the creditor's consent is absolutely essential (Art. 1293, Civil Code). Don't think you can escape corporate liabilities by just transferring all assets to another company, especially if the new company continues the business, as the "business-enterprise transfer" rule might make them liable too.

## Module 3B. Fraud, Negligence, and Delay

### Stolt-Nielsen Transportation Group, Inc. v. Medequillo, Jr.

Summary: Stolt-Nielsen hired Medequillo for a ship but sent him home early. To "placate" him, they offered a new contract for a different vessel, but then failed to deploy him. When Medequillo sued, Stolt-Nielsen argued his claim for the first contract was time-barred. The Supreme Court ruled that the second contract *novated* the first, making Stolt-Nielsen liable for breaching the *second* contract by not deploying Medequillo.

LL: Always remember that a new, incompatible contract can extinguish an old one through novation, even if not explicitly stated. Don't assume an old obligation is simply gone; if you create a new one to replace it, you're bound by the new terms. This applies under Civil Code Article 1292, where obligations are incompatible.

### The Swedish East Asia Co., Ltd. v. Manila Port Service

Summary: Swedish East Asia Co.'s ship mistakenly unloaded steel bundles meant for Hongkong at Manila Port Service (MPS). When Swedish East Asia tried to retrieve them, 8 bundles were missing. MPS argued its management contract's short prescriptive period applied and that Swedish East Asia, an unlicensed foreign company, couldn't sue. The Supreme Court said the contract didn't apply because this was a mistaken delivery (*solutio indebiti*), making MPS liable to return the goods. The Court also ruled that an unlicensed foreign corporation can sue for an "isolated transaction."

LL: Don't assume contractual limitations apply to every situation, especially if the obligation arises from a mistake. Always remember that *solutio indebiti* (Civil Code Art. 2154) creates an obligation to return unduly received goods, bypassing specific contract terms. Make sure to distinguish between "doing business" and "isolated transactions" for foreign corporations' capacity to sue.

### Moreño-Lentfer v. Wolff

Summary: Hans Jurgen Wolff, a German national, trusted Victoria Moreño-Lentfer and her husband to help him buy a beach house and lease rights from John Cross. Wolff paid the full amount, but Victoria and Cross secretly executed a deed of sale making it appear Victoria bought the property for a much lower price. When Wolff discovered the fraud, he sued. The Supreme Court ruled that Victoria was unjustly enriched and ordered the property reconveyed to Wolff, rejecting claims that Wolff's payment was a donation or that alien land ownership was violated.

LL: Don't trust blindly in property transactions; always ensure all agreements are clear and properly documented. Make sure any significant donation (over P5,000) is in writing, as required by Art. 748 of the Civil Code, otherwise it's void. The court will prevent unjust enrichment (Art. 22) and *solutio indebiti* (Art. 2154) when someone benefits from fraud at another's expense.

## Module 3C. Stipulations and Conditions

### Hyatt Elevators and Escalators Corp. v. Cathedral Heights Building Complex

Summary: Hyatt Elevators installed expensive replacement parts in Cathedral Heights' elevators without getting proper purchase orders or board approval, claiming urgency. Cathedral Heights refused to pay the P1.16 million bill, arguing Hyatt bypassed their standard operating procedures and unilaterally set the price. The Supreme Court sided with Cathedral Heights, ruling there was no perfected contract of sale because there was no "meeting of the minds" on the price. Hyatt got nothing.

LL: Always follow your client's established procedures for approvals and pricing, even in urgent situations. Don't assume signatures on delivery receipts mean consent to a unilaterally fixed price. A contract of sale requires a "meeting of the minds" on the price (Art. 1318, 1458 Civil Code), otherwise, you might not be able to collect.

### National Power Corporation v. Benguet Electric Cooperative (BENECO)

Summary: NPC messed up its metering system, underbilling BENECO for four years. When NPC finally realized its mistake, it demanded BENECO pay back over P157 million, arguing unjust enrichment. BENECO refused, saying their contract had a strict 90-day period for correcting billing errors. The Supreme Court agreed with BENECO, ruling that NPC's gross negligence meant it couldn't invoke unjust enrichment to bypass its own contract, but BENECO still had to pay for the last 90 days.

LL: Always honor your contracts; they have the force of law (Art. 1159). Don't rely on the principle of unjust enrichment (accion in rem verso) if your contract already provides a specific remedy for a situation, as it's only an auxiliary action. Public utilities are held to a high standard of care and diligence.

## Module 4A. Contracts in General

### Asentista v. Jupp & Company, Inc.

Summary: Marilyn Asentista, a sales agent for Jupp & Company, was promised a 2% commission on top of her salary and used a company car for work. When she resigned, Jupp refused to pay her commissions and unilaterally deducted "car plan" and "rental" fees from her unpaid wages, even though there was no prior agreement for these deductions. The Supreme Court sided with Asentista, saying Jupp couldn't just deduct money without an agreement and had to pay her earned commissions.

LL: Always make sure all terms, especially commissions and company benefits like car plans, are clearly written down. Don't assume anything. Employers have the burden to prove they paid employees, not the other way around. Unilateral deductions or withholding pay without a clear agreement is unjust enrichment under Article 22 of the Civil Code.

### Jandoc-Gatdula v. Dimalanta

Summary: Manuela Jandoc, a B'laan, sold land to Vicenta Natividad in 1948, but the sale was technically void because it lacked government approval for sales by cultural minorities. Despite this, Manuela accepted payment and gave Vicenta possession, even promising to formalize the sale later. Decades later, Manuela's heir, Catalina, tried to reclaim the land, arguing the sale was void. The Supreme Court said Catalina couldn't recover it because Manuela had voluntarily performed and waited too long.

LL: Don't wait decades to assert your rights, even if a contract is technically void. The Court won't let you reclaim something if you voluntarily performed your part and then slept on your rights for too long. This case shows how equity, through laches and estoppel, can prevent recovery, similar to how natural obligations (Art. 1423) make voluntary payments irrecoverable.

### Philippine Stock Exchange, Inc. v. Litonjua

Summary: The Litonjua Group wanted to buy a trading seat from Trendline Securities. PSE told Litonjua they'd accept P19M to settle Trendline's debts, which would then unencumber the seat for transfer. Litonjua paid PSE the P19M, but PSE never lifted the seat's suspension, making the transfer impossible. When Litonjua demanded a refund, PSE refused, claiming they weren't directly involved. The Supreme Court ordered PSE to refund the P19M, finding them unjustly enriched and estopped from denying their commitment.

LL: Don't accept money for a specific purpose if you can't or won't fulfill that purpose, even if you're not a direct party to the main contract. Always remember that your conduct, like accepting payment under specific conditions, can create an obligation based on unjust enrichment (Article 22, Civil Code) and estoppel, preventing you from denying your role.

## Module 4B. Defective Contracts

### Philippine Realty and Holdings Corp. v. Ley Construction and Development Corp.

Summary: Philippine Realty (PRHC) hired Ley Construction (LCDC) to build Tektite Towers. When material costs soared, PRHC's managers promised LCDC a P36M escalation and waived delay penalties if LCDC advanced funds. LCDC poured P38M of its own money into the project, relying on these assurances. Later, PRHC denied the escalation, claiming its board never approved it, and demanded delay penalties. The Supreme Court ruled PRHC was estopped, as it benefited from LCDC's reliance and its own silence, ordering PRHC to pay.

LL: Don't stay silent when you know another party is relying on your (or your agent's) representations, especially if you're benefiting. Always ensure your corporate representatives have clear authority, or you might be bound by their apparent authority. Estoppel (Art. 1431) prevents you from denying a representation if someone relied on it to their detriment.

### F.A.T. Kee Computer Systems, Inc. v. Online Networks International, Inc.

Summary: F.A.T. Kee bought computers from Online Networks, with payment due in US dollars. They argued over the peso exchange rate, with Online Networks first sending an SOA at P34:US$1, then demanding P40. F.A.T. Kee offered P37:US$1, and Online Networks accepted subsequent payments without protest. The Supreme Court ruled that Online Networks was estopped from demanding a higher rate than P37 because its silence and acceptance of payments implied agreement.

LL: Don't stay silent and accept benefits if you disagree with an offer, especially in a contract dispute. Your inaction, coupled with accepting payments, can bind you to the other party's terms through estoppel (Article 1431). Make sure to clearly reject offers you don't agree with to protect your rights.

### Pureza v. Court of Appeals

Summary: Ruperto Pureza got a housing loan from Asia Trust Bank for his house, built by Spouses Alejandro. Pureza signed a Certificate of House Completion and an Order of Payment, authorizing the bank to release funds to the contractors. Later, Pureza sued the bank, claiming the house was incomplete and the bank was negligent. The Supreme Court ruled Pureza couldn't blame the bank because he himself certified the completion, estopping him from denying it.

LL: Don't sign documents like "Certificate of Completion" if the work isn't actually done or if you don't fully agree. Your own written representations can bind you, preventing you from later claiming otherwise. This is based on the doctrine of estoppel (Article 1431, Civil Code), which stops you from going back on your word if someone relied on it in good faith.
