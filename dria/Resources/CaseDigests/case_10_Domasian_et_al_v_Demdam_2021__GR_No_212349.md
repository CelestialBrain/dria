# Case 10: Domasian, et al. v. Demdam (2021) | G.R. No. 212349

# A Comprehensive Legal Analysis of Domasian, et al. v. Demdam (2021)

**Key Points:**
*   It appears that the core dispute involves the collection of a P75,000 loan with a stipulated 8% monthly interest rate, which the Supreme Court ultimately deemed unconscionable and reduced to 12% per annum.
*   Research suggests that the Regional Trial Court (RTC) correctly exercised jurisdiction over the case because monetary interest—being a primary and inseparable component of the cause of action—must be included in computing the jurisdictional amount, bringing the total claim to P489,000.
*   While the debtors attempted to pay the debt in 2010 via a Manager's Check, the evidence leans toward the conclusion that this mere tender of payment, without subsequent judicial consignation, was legally insufficient to suspend the accrual of interest or extinguish the obligation.
*   Although discussions surrounding Civil Code Articles 1240 and 1241 (payment to the wrong party) frequently arise in extinguishment of obligations, the facts of this case confirm that the payment was tendered to the proper creditor. The fatal defect lay in the procedure of payment rather than the identity of the payee.

## Introduction to the Legal Doctrines at Issue
The extinguishment of obligations through payment or performance is a cornerstone of civil law, primarily governed by Articles 1232 through 1261 of the Civil Code of the Philippines. For a payment to be legally valid and thus extinguish an obligation, it must meet rigorous standards regarding identity, integrity, and the propriety of both the payor and the payee. Furthermore, when a creditor unjustly refuses to accept a validly tendered payment, the law provides the debtor with the remedy of consignation to avoid the perpetual accrual of interest and the enduring burden of the debt. The Supreme Court decision in *Spouses Domasian v. Demdam* (G.R. No. 212349, November 17, 2021) offers a critical examination of these principles, specifically illustrating the intersection between substantive civil law obligations, procedural jurisdictional rules, and the equitable power of the courts to temper unconscionable interest rates. 

## Case Context and Methodological Scope
This report synthesizes the factual background, procedural history, and substantive rulings of the aforementioned case. It specifically addresses the user's inquiry regarding the identity of the proper payee and the consequences of defective payment. Notably, while the query contemplates the application of Civil Code Articles 1240 and 1241 (pertaining to the proper party to receive payment), an exhaustive review of the case reveals that the critical failure in the debtors' performance was not a misdirection of payment to a third or wrong party, but rather a failure to consummate a valid tender of payment with the requisite judicial consignation under Articles 1256 to 1261.

### Domasian, et al. v. Demdam (2021) | G.R. No. 212349

#### 1. The Parties

*   **Petitioners (Domasian, et al.):** The petitioners are Spouses Sergio D. Domasian and Nenita F. Domasian. They acted as the borrowers (debtors) in a contract of loan (mutuum). Their primary obligation was to return the principal amount borrowed along with the stipulated monetary interest upon the maturity date of the loan.
*   **Respondent (Demdam):** The respondent is Manuel T. Demdam, who acted as the lender (creditor) in the transaction. He initiated the legal action to collect the unpaid sum of money, claiming entitlement not only to the principal amount but also to an extensive sum of accrued interest based on the originally agreed-upon contractual rate.
*   **Their relationship:** The underlying relationship between the parties was founded upon a straightforward contract of loan (mutuum) executed on October 30, 1995. Under this contract, the creditor delivered money to the debtors, creating a unilateral obligation on the part of the debtors to pay the principal loan and the accrued interest by a specified maturity date.

#### 2. Background & Facts

The factual matrix of this case spans over two decades, evolving from a simple loan default into a complex procedural and substantive legal battle involving default judgments, jurisdictional disputes, and attempts at payment.

*   **The Original Obligation:** The original obligation was a contract of loan for the principal purchase/borrowed amount of P75,000.00. The parties stipulated an exceedingly high interest rate of 8% per month (equivalent to 96% per annum), with a maturity date set for June 30, 1996.
*   **To Whom Payment Was Made (and Proper Payee Status):** When the debt matured, the petitioners initially defaulted. Years later, during the pendency of the litigation (specifically sometime in 2010), the petitioners attempted to settle the obligation. They prepared a Manager's Check and offered it directly to the respondent, Manuel T. Demdam. Demdam was undoubtedly the proper payee, as he was the person in whose favor the obligation was constituted. No third party or wrong payee was involved in this transaction.
*   **The Creditor's Claim:** The actual creditor (Demdam) claimed that the obligation remained fundamentally unpaid for 19 years since the loan was obtained. He refused to accept the Manager's Check tendered by the petitioners in 2010. The creditor's core claim in his lawsuit was not that payment was made to the wrong person, but rather that the total debt (principal plus 8% monthly interest) had not been satisfied, prompting his collection suit for P489,000.00.

#### 3. The Contract or Obligation

*   **Original Obligation:** The underlying obligation was a mutuum (simple loan) for a principal sum of P75,000.00 subject to a conventional (monetary) interest rate of 8% per month.
*   **Extinguishment by Payment:** The obligation was **not** extinguished by payment prior to the litigation or during the litigation. The payment attempted by the debtors in 2010 was not legally valid to extinguish the obligation. 
*   **Tender of Payment and Consignation:** There was a **tender of payment** made by the petitioners in 2010 when they offered a Manager's Check to the respondent. However, this tender was **not proper** to produce the legal effect of payment. The Supreme Court expressly noted that while tender of payment manifests a desire to comply with the obligation, it produces no legal effect on its own. To validly extinguish an obligation and suspend the accrual of interest, the tender of payment, if refused without just cause, must be followed by a prompt and valid **consignation** (the deposit of the proper amount with a judicial authority). Because the petitioners failed to consign the amount with the court after Demdam refused the check, the tender was legally insufficient.

#### 4. Legal Issues Raised

*   **Was payment made to the proper payee under Art. 1240?** Yes. Civil Code Article 1240 mandates that payment shall be made to the person in whose favor the obligation has been constituted. In this case, the petitioners directly offered the Manager's Check to the respondent, Manuel T. Demdam, who was the original creditor. The defect in this case was not a violation of Article 1240. 
*   **If payment was to a third party, was it valid under Art. 1241?** This legal issue is inapplicable to the specific facts of this case. The comprehensive text of the ruling confirms that payment was never offered to an incapacitated person or an unauthorized third party. Therefore, Article 1241 was not invoked or violated.
*   **Was the obligation extinguished?** No. The core issue regarding payment was whether the unaccepted tender of a Manager's Check suspended the accrual of interest. The Court ruled it did not, because tender of payment without consignation does not extinguish the obligation.
*   **Additional Primary Issues:** 
    1. Did the RTC have jurisdiction over the claim considering the principal was only P75,000.00?
    2. Was the stipulated interest rate of 8% per month legally valid?.

#### 5. Supreme Court Ruling

The Supreme Court fundamentally ruled in favor of the creditor's right to collect but heavily tempered the terms of the collection based on equitable principles.

*   **On the Validity of Payment (Tender & Consignation):** The Supreme Court decided that the 2010 tender of payment was legally defective. Citing *Spouses Bonrostro v. Spouses Luna*, the Court held that tender of payment without more produces no legal effect. Because the petitioners failed to execute the companion act of consignation (depositing the refused funds with the proper court), the accrual of interest on their loan obligation was not suspended, and the obligation was not extinguished.
*   **Application of Civil Code Provisions:** The Court did not apply Articles 1240–1241, as the identity of the payee was never in dispute. Instead, the Court implicitly applied the principles of Articles 1256 to 1261 governing Tender of Payment and Consignation. Furthermore, the Court exercised its power under the Civil Code (referencing principles aligned with Art. 1956 and related jurisprudence) to strike down the 8% monthly interest rate (96% per annum) as "excessive, iniquitous, unconscionable and exorbitant," thus rendering it void *ab initio*.
*   **On Jurisdiction:** The Court ruled that the RTC had proper jurisdiction. Under the law, "monetary interest" (which is agreed upon as compensation for the use of money) is a primary and inseparable component of the principal cause of action. Therefore, the P414,000.00 in accrued interest was correctly added to the P75,000.00 principal, bringing the total claim to P489,000.00, well within the RTC's jurisdictional threshold at the time of filing in 2001.
*   **Disposition (The Fallo):** The Supreme Court affirmed the CA's decision to reinstate the RTC's judgment but heavily modified the financial awards. The petitioners were ordered to pay:
    1. The P75,000.00 principal.
    2. Monetary interest at the reduced legal rate of 12% per annum from extrajudicial demand (June 30, 1996) until finality.
    3. Compensatory interest on the accrued monetary interest at 12% per annum from judicial demand (August 1, 2001) to June 30, 2013, and 6% per annum thereafter until finality.
    4. Legal interest of 6% per annum on the total aggregate amount from the finality of the decision until full payment.
    5. The awards for moral and exemplary damages were deleted due to a lack of evidence of fraud or bad faith.

#### 6. Key Doctrine / Civil Code Hook

*   **The Rule on Valid Payment (Arts. 1240–1241 vs. Arts. 1256–1261):** While Articles 1240 and 1241 dictate *to whom* payment must be made (the creditor, his successor, or an authorized person), this case turns on the *method* of payment when faced with creditor refusal. The fundamental doctrine is that **tender of payment must be followed by valid consignation** to produce the effect of payment and extinguish the obligation.
*   **What this case adds or clarifies:** 
    1. **Tender vs. Consignation:** The case strictly reiterates that a debtor cannot simply offer a check, have it refused, and then claim that interest should stop accruing. A "prompt attempt of the debtor to deposit the means of payment in court by way of consignation" is mandatory.
    2. **Jurisdiction Computation:** It clarifies the distinction between monetary interest and compensatory interest for jurisdictional purposes. Contractual monetary interest is added to the principal to determine the jurisdictional amount; compensatory damages are excluded.
    3. **Unconscionable Interest:** It reinforces the doctrine that while parties are free to stipulate interest, courts will void rates that are shockingly high (e.g., 8% a month) and replace them with the prevailing legal rate at the time of the contract.
*   **Civil Code Articles Applied:** 
    *   **Art. 1256:** (Implicitly applied regarding the necessity of consignation when the creditor refuses tender).
    *   **Art. 1956:** (Related to the imposition of monetary interest).
    *   **Art. 2212:** (Interest due shall earn legal interest from the time it is judicially demanded).

#### 7. Memory Anchor

**"A rejected check does not stop the clock."** 
In *Domasian*, the debtors tried to hand the creditor a check to pay off their P75,000 loan, but because they simply walked away when he refused it instead of depositing it with the court (*consignation*), the 19 years of interest (though mercifully reduced from a predatory 96% to 12% by the SC) continued to tick against them.
